## I. Theoretical Foundations in Religious Studies Literature

### A. Defining Divine Intervention Across Traditions

The academic literature presents varying definitions of divine intervention, with most scholars agreeing on core phenomenological characteristics. There are three types of divine intervention based on this view: Big showy miracles, ordinary displays of God’s mercy, and one act of love so powerful it gave us the reason for our faith. This classification system provides a useful framework for categorizing manifestations across different religious contexts.

## II. Scientific and Medical Literature

### A. Medical Documentation of Miraculous Healings

Recent medical literature has begun acknowledging unexplained healing phenomena. Duffin’s study is good empirical history. She presents ample case narratives involving cancer, blindness, lung conditions and other debilitating diseases. This represents a significant shift toward academic legitimization of miracle investigation.

**Key Medical Research Findings:**

- Global Medical Research Institute (GMRI) has recently published a peer-reviewed case report of blindness healed through prayer. We have very strong medical evidence of a miracle here
- Documented cases include: juvenile macular degeneration reversals, cancer remissions, organ function restoration
- Medical institutions are developing protocols for documenting unexplained recoveries
- randomized controlled clinical trials that have shown improvement in health outcomes following in-person prayer

### B. Documentation Challenges and Solutions

The medical community faces significant challenges in miracle documentation. doctors are not in the business of documenting miracles. The term “miracle” does not belong in medical vocabulary. However, organizations like the Global Medical Research Institute are developing systematic approaches to overcome these barriers.

**Established Documentation Protocols:**

- Before-and-after medical record compilation
- Multiple physician verification requirements
- Standardized diagnostic criteria for “inexplicable” classifications
- Long-term follow-up studies to confirm permanent healing
